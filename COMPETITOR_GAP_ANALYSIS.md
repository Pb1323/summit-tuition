# 11+ Market — Competitor Research & Gap Analysis

Last updated: 2026-07-10. Web research into online mock platforms, local/franchise tutoring, and community-targeted competitors, with gaps mapped against what Summit Tuition already does or could build.

## Market context

- 29% of UK 11-16 year olds now get private tuition outside school (up from 18% twenty years ago), rising to ~50% in London.
- Grammar school competition is intensifying — partly driven by private-school VAT pushing families toward the state grammar route. In hotspots like Golders Green, ~1,200 children apply for ~300 places at a single school.
- Typical tutor rates: £30-50/hr nationally, £45-75/hr in London hotspots.
- Online tutoring market growing ~16.8% CAGR — this is a genuinely expanding market, not a zero-sum one.

**Local/franchise in-person providers:**
- **Kumon** — 600+ UK centres, £65/subject/month, but general maths/English, not 11+-specialised, individualised worksheets not exam-format practice.
- **Explore Learning** — 124 centres, offers "11+ guidance" alongside general tutoring, not a dedicated 11+ specialist.
- Mumsnet complaints specifically about **group tuition**: "awful," "like an extension of school," "total waste of money" — group format has a real trust problem.

**Community/ethnicity-targeted:**
- **11 Plus Tutor UK** is the one competitor explicitly marketing to this niche — its own copy targets families with "Asian genes" (Indian subcontinent, Korean, Chinese), from £8/hr, claims 98% pass rate. The framing is crude and could easily alienate as many parents as it attracts.
- Beyond that, community targeting is essentially just generic Mandarin/language tutors (Manhattan Mandarin, Titanium Tutors, Connexcel) who aren't 11+-specialists at all.

## Where the gaps are

1. **Trust deficit is real and specific, not vague.** Verified Trustpilot complaints: access cut off on the day of a mock, payments routed to a different company than advertised, tutors giving factually wrong answers, offshore-outsourced markers with communication issues. Summit's manual admin approval + safeguarding messaging + "results stay inside the platform" model directly answers this — worth leading with in marketing copy, not burying it.

2. **VR/NVR content is a documented market-wide weak spot**, independently confirmed by research ("limited resources which offer extensive practice on all the sections," hard to target a child's specific weak pattern-family) — and it's also literally the weakest part of Summit's own current mock library (`vr-placeholder`/`nvr-placeholder` are unpublished, zero questions). This is the single highest-leverage content gap to fill: it's both a proven market gap and your own biggest current hole.

3. **No one blends affordable online mocks + optional real human tuition + manual marking in one product.** The market splits cleanly into (a) pure online platforms with auto-marked/AI feedback, or (b) pure in-person franchises doing generic tutoring, not 11+-specific exam simulation. Summit's existing plan tiers (diagnostic → mock club → group/private tuition) already span this — but nothing in the current marketing copy states "we're the only one doing both" explicitly.

4. **Parent-readable reports are a stated pain point**, not just a nice-to-have — research on school reporting broadly confirms parents want jargon-free "here's what to fix next," not a wall of data. Summit's existing topic-breakdown/weak-topics report already does the right thing structurally; worth QA-ing that the actual copy avoids jargon, since this is a differentiator you can prove with a sample report on the marketing site.

5. **Community targeting whitespace exists but needs a better angle than the current market leader's.** 11 Plus Tutor UK owns the "targets Asian families" positioning with language that risks feeling reductive. A version built on genuine relationships (the Chinese-school/Hindu-temple outreach from the earlier marketing research) rather than ethnicity-as-a-selling-point in the ad copy itself is a real, occupyable gap — respectful positioning, not exploitative.

6. **Hyperlocal SEO is being done by at least one competitor (TutorLab)** with location-specific landing pages ("11 Plus tutors in Wilmslow," "...in Golders Green," "...in Putney"). Confirms the programmatic local-landing-page idea from the earlier SEO work is a validated tactic, not a guess — worth prioritising areas with known grammar-school demand pockets (Golders Green, Kent, Bucks catchments).

7. **Pricing positioning**: Atom is expensive and gates mocks behind its top tier; EdifyPod undercuts everyone at <£20/mo with more boards covered. A pure race-to-the-bottom on price against EdifyPod is a losing game — the more defensible angle is trust/human-marking/transparency (gap #1) rather than being the cheapest.

## Bottom line — where to focus first
**VR/NVR content build-out** is the strongest opportunity: it's simultaneously a validated market-wide gap, your own biggest current product hole, and something no amount of marketing spend fixes for a competitor overnight. Pair it with **explicit trust-focused messaging** (manual marking, no access-cutoff risk, transparent single-company billing) since that's a documented, named pain point competitors are actively failing on right now, per real reviews.
