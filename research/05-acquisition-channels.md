# 05 — Acquisition Channels: How UK Tutoring Businesses Get Their First Customers

*Research for Summit Tuition (Oxford, premium 11+ tuition + GL-style mock exams, pre-launch, zero customers). July 2026.*

**Headline finding:** the UK 11+ market runs almost entirely on parent-to-parent recommendation inside a famously closed, hyper-active parent network (school-gate conversations, WhatsApp/Facebook groups, the Eleven Plus Exams forum, Mumsnet). Every channel below should be judged by one question: *does it manufacture the first recommendations, or does it depend on them already existing?* At zero customers, the channels that manufacture proof (mock exam days, marketplace reviews, a first partner school) beat the channels that broadcast (ads, leaflets, Instagram).

---

## 1. What the founder stories say about "zero to first customers"

### Atom Learning — the most relevant case study
Atom Learning (UK's largest 11+/entrance-exam edtech, now 90,000+ children) is the closest analogue to Summit. Its early acquisition sequence, per co-founder Alex Hatvany:

- **Started as a tutoring agency, not a platform**: the founders "put flyers through letterboxes in affluent parts of London every weekend" to get the first tutoring clients, using unemployed Oxford-graduate friends as tutors ([UKTN founder interview](https://www.uktech.news/news/founder-interviews/alex-hatvany-founder-atom-learning-20221209)).
- **The inflection point was institutional, not consumer**: "Signing up our first school was key — it gave us credibility with surrounding schools and from there the growth has been exponential" ([Startups.co.uk](https://startups.co.uk/just-started/atom-learning-jake-okeeffe-and-alex-hatvany/)).
- They bootstrapped to profitability for three years before raising a $25M SoftBank Series A ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2021/12/09/uks-atom-learning-picks-up-25m-from-softbank-for-an-ai-based-online-learning-platform-aimed-at-elementary-school-aged-students/)).

**Lesson:** even a venture-scale 11+ platform began with grubby local hustle plus one credibility-granting institution. Consumer brand advertising came much later.

### Small UK tutoring businesses
- **Church Langley Tutoring (Crawley)**, founded by a 17-year-old, grew to 70+ students "primarily through referrals, repeat families and word-of-mouth recommendations, with many parents later enrolling younger siblings" ([Sussex Express](https://www.sussexexpress.co.uk/your-world/crawley-business-founder-shortlisted-for-uk-award-after-building-high-demand-tutoring-service-at-17-8641098)).
- On Mumsnet's tutor threads, the consistent pattern from working tutors: initial trickles from directories/local ads, then "all regular students ultimately come through word of mouth"; one tutor reported it took until year 4 of running courses to get steady repeat customers and unsolicited recommendations ([Mumsnet — "fellow tutors, where do you advertise?"](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/tutoring/5060567-fellow-tutors-where-do-you-advertise)).
- The Tutors' Association's own marketing guidance leads with the same message: "Happy students and parents are your best marketing tool" — actively engineer reviews, referrals and testimonials from day one; use credibility markers (membership register, professional profile) to compensate for having no track record ([The Tutors' Association](https://thetutorsassociation.org.uk/2025/03/13/how-tutors-can-market-themselves-effectively-building-a-successful-tutoring-business/)).

**Implication for a zero-customer business:** word of mouth is the destination, not the starting channel. The first-customer job is to *seed* it artificially — free/cheap high-value experiences, borrowed credibility, and asking explicitly for referrals after every early win.

## 2. The 11+ parent network: unusually dense, unusually closed

The 11+ segment has a parent-community culture that most tutoring niches lack — this is Summit's single biggest structural advantage *and* barrier.

- Parent-founded 11+ Facebook groups have "tens of thousands of members between them and are growing"; examples include *11+ for the Sutton Schools* (~1,000 members) and regional prep groups with 1,200+ ([Grammar School Insider](https://grammarschoolinsider.com/2020/07/15/tapping-the-social-network-for-11-plus-advice/)).
- 11+ WhatsApp advice groups exist and at least one hit WhatsApp's then-250-member cap; parents report that in any group over ~120 members "you can source pretty much anything, including a maths tutor" ([Grammar School Insider](https://grammarschoolinsider.com/2020/07/15/tapping-the-social-network-for-11-plus-advice/); [Harper's Bazaar Arabia on parent WhatsApp culture](https://www.harpersbazaararabia.com/culture/junior/parent-whatsapp-groups)).
- The [Eleven Plus Exams forum](https://www.elevenplusexams.co.uk/forum/11plus/) — the UK's largest 11+ community — confirms "the most common source of an 11+ tutor is personal recommendation from parents at the school gates who have already gone through the 11+"; and notably, "in some areas the 11+ is so fiercely competitive that a **conspiracy of silence** surrounds the identities of the best tutors" ([finding-a-tutor guide](https://www.elevenplusexams.co.uk/services/11plus-tutors/finding-a-tutor/)). The forum only allows tutor recommendations by private message.
- Mumsnet 11+ threads show the demand-side behaviour: parents asking for "secret" tutors by postcode, being told to "ask parents with kids in the year above," and reporting tutors with waiting lists stretching to 2027 ([Mumsnet — secret 11+ tutors thread](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/primary/3308264-Can-anyone-send-me-details-of-the-secret-11plus-tutors-Twickenham-teddington-Chiswick-Kingston-or-nearby-area); [Mumsnet — ideal time to get an 11+ tutor](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/secondary/4805675-ideal-time-to-get-an-11-tutor)).

**Two practical consequences:**
1. You cannot cold-post your way into these groups — self-promotion is usually banned and distrusted ("beware… a recommendation may have a vested interest" — elevenplusexams moderators). You need real parents mentioning you organically, which loops back to seeding proof.
2. Once even 3–5 parents in a group vouch for you, the density of these networks makes growth compounding — waiting lists are the norm for trusted 11+ providers.

**Oxford nuance:** Oxfordshire itself is comprehensive (no state grammars); the local 11+ market is (a) Bucks/Reading/Slough grammar commuters and (b) Oxford independents — Magdalen College School, Oxford High GDST (Quest/ISEB-style adaptive tests), Headington, Abingdon ([Exam Papers Plus on Oxford High admissions](https://exampapersplus.co.uk/advice/11-plus-year-6/oxford-high-school-11-plus-exam-selective-admissions-assessments-information/); [11plustutoring.uk Oxfordshire page](https://www.11plustutoring.uk/11-plus-exams/county/11-plus-tutoring-oxfordshire/)). Local channels: [Oxfordshire Mummies](https://oxfordshiremummies.co.uk/) aggregates the local parent-group ecosystem; the [Good Schools Guide's Oxford tutors page](https://www.goodschoolsguide.co.uk/best-schools/oxford-and-oxfordshire/tutors) notes Oxford parents lean on independent tutors found by word of mouth, with Kip McGrath and three Kumon centres as the franchise presence — i.e. no dominant local 11+ mock brand. That's a gap.

## 3. Mock exam days as an acquisition funnel — the proven 11+ wedge

Paid mock exam days are an established, standalone product in the 11+ world, and they double as the best-documented acquisition funnel in the niche:

- **Chuckra 11+** runs national mock test days (May–August, booking opens March), four distinct paper sets per season so children attend up to four times; OMR-marked, audio-timed, with wrong answers feeding a "retry" bank that pulls families onto its online platform ([Chuckra mock days](https://www.11plus.co.uk/mock-exam-days-2026/); [FAQ](https://www.11plus.co.uk/mock-test-day-faq/)).
- **Susan Daughtrey Education** and **Marie Redmond Tuition** are regarded as market leaders in Bucks/Berks — SDE mocks are prized for being "closest in content, timing, format, administration and difficulty" to the real test, sold per target school (Reading Boys, Latymer, etc.) and parents on Mumsnet actively debate which provider's mocks to book, often booking several ([SDE mock shop](https://susandaughtreyeducation.com/mock_shop); [Mumsnet — which mocks to choose](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/secondary/5240120-11-plus-mocks-which-one-to-chosemarie-remond-or-susan-daughtery-or-examberry); [11plusguide list of Bucks mock providers](https://www.11plusguide.com/11-plus-exam-preparation/11-plus-mock-exams/11-plus-mock-exams-buckinghamshire/)).
- Providers explicitly bridge mock → tuition: Riviera Tuition and Marie Redmond sell mocks alongside courses; marked papers come back with tutor feedback (some via video) within days — a natural upsell moment ([Riviera Tuition mocks](https://rivieratuition.co.uk/private-tuition/11-plus-tuition/11-plus-mock-exams/); [Marie Redmond](https://marieredmond.co.uk/)).
- Demand is so strong that parents even enter children for the *real* Kent Test as a practice run — "test tourism" costing grammar schools money ([Schools Week](https://schoolsweek.co.uk/grammar-school-coffers-drained-by-test-tourism-as-pushy-parents-use-11-plus-as-mock-exam/)). Parents will pay for realistic exam simulation; supply of credible GL-style mock days sells out regionally.
- The free-tier version of the same funnel is ubiquitous online: 11 Plus Guru and others use free online mocks/assessments as the lead magnet into paid tuition ([11 Plus Guru free mocks](https://www.11plusguru.com/free-online-mock-exams)); franchises like Kumon are built on the "free assessment → enrol" mechanic ([Kumon free assessment](https://www.kumon.co.uk/book-now)).

**Why this is the ideal zero-customer move for Summit:** a mock day is a *transactional first purchase* that doesn't require trust in your teaching (parents buy the exam experience, not you); it delivers a data-rich report that demonstrates your platform; it puts 30–60 target-market families in a room (or on the platform) at once; and every attendee's result report is a personalised sales document for tuition. It also generates the first Google/Trustpilot reviews and the first parent-group mentions. Nobody currently owns "the Oxford 11+ mock day" the way SDE owns Bucks.

## 4. Local tactics: what the ground game looks like

Evidence from tutor threads and small-business forums:

- **Local parent Facebook groups** are repeatedly cited by working tutors as one of the few advertising channels that actually produced bookings — posting (where rules allow) in "local mums" groups, plus leaving leaflets in coffee shops *with permission*, and donating raffle prizes to primary school PTAs in exchange for logo placement on posters ([Mumsnet tutor advertising thread](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/tutoring/5060567-fellow-tutors-where-do-you-advertise)).
- **PTA sponsorship** is a legitimised route into school communities: PTAs actively seek local business sponsors and repay them with thank-yous in the school/PTA newsletter, social channels and event publicity — i.e. paid-for word of mouth inside the exact audience ([PTA+ on business partnerships](https://www.pta.co.uk/running-a-pta/community-engagement/building-business-partnerships/)). Offering tiered options ("they'll more likely pick one than say no") is the recommended ask ([PTA+ promoting partnerships](https://www.pta.co.uk/partnerships/business/promoting-partnerships/)).
- **Nextdoor** functions as a recommendations engine (users ask for and get service recommendations); several tutoring businesses maintain pages, but evidence of it driving tutoring volume is thin — treat as free hygiene, not a strategy ([Mumsnet on Nextdoor](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/_chat/3582612-Has-anybody-bothered-to-join-Nextdoor-co-uk); [a tutor page on Nextdoor](https://nextdoor.co.uk/pages/the-tutor-group/)).
- **School gates — do it as a parent, not a promoter.** Direct leafleting outside schools backfires: schools "react strongly negatively when people try to sell things outside school gates," and unapproved leaflets reaching children create safeguarding-adjacent friction ([UK Business Forums](https://www.ukbusinessforums.co.uk/threads/handing-out-leaflets-to-students-outside-of-secondary-schools.366343/)). The school-gate channel that works is *conversation between parents* — which you influence via delighted early customers, not flyers.
- **Libraries/community noticeboards** get recommended in generic marketing guides ([TutorCruncher](https://tutorcruncher.com/tutoring-online/where-to-advertise-tutoring-services/)) but no tutor in the threads reviewed attributed actual clients to them. Low cost, low yield; fine as background presence for a premium brand only if the materials look premium.

## 5. Marketplaces and lead-gen sites as a seeding channel

Take-rates are painful but at zero customers the platforms are buying you your first reviews and reference families:

| Platform | Cut / cost | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Tutorful | ~25–35% effective (35% service fee added on top of tutor rate) | tutors report frustration but steady student flow ([PMT fees explainer](https://www.pmt.education/blog/tutors/tutoring-platform-fees-and-commissions-explained/); [Trustpilot](https://uk.trustpilot.com/review/tutorful.co.uk)) |
| MyTutor | ~40% + VAT commission (tutor keeps roughly half) | ([PMT fees explainer](https://www.pmt.education/blog/tutors/tutoring-platform-fees-and-commissions-explained/); [GoStudent comparison](https://www.gostudent.org/en-gb/blog/mytutor-vs-superprof-comparison)) |
| Superprof | ~10% via payment processor; new tutors effectively give first lesson free until they earn a review | ([Sidehustles review](https://sidehustles.com/superprof-tutoring-review/)) |
| Bark | Pay-per-lead (£4–£30/lead in credits); widespread complaints of stale/fake leads — "over 75% of leads who answer claim they didn't inquire" | one Mumsnet tutor called Bark their best client source, so results vary ([Sidehusl](https://sidehusl.com/bark/); [Sidehustles Bark review](https://sidehustles.com/bark-com-review/); [Mumsnet](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/tutoring/5060567-fellow-tutors-where-do-you-advertise)) |

**Strategic read for Summit:** marketplaces sell *individual tutor hours*, which conflicts with a premium platform brand and cedes the client relationship. Use them, if at all, only as a short bridge: a founder-tutor profile on Superprof/Tutorful to land 5–10 Oxford 11+ families, deliver exceptional results, then migrate goodwill (reviews, testimonials, referrals) to the Summit brand. First Tutors and the [elevenplusexams tutor directory](https://www.elevenplusexams.co.uk/services/11plus-tutors/finding-a-tutor/) (unvetted, cheap listing) are worth a listing purely for discoverability, since Mumsnet parents name-check them when searching ([Mumsnet — how to find a tutor](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/tutoring/5132343-how-to-find-a-tutor)).

## 6. Paid channels: costs and honest expectations

- **Google Ads**: UK tutoring-adjacent CPCs run roughly £2.50–£3.50+, with competitive tutoring keywords around the £3.50 mark and bids for top positions ranging much higher ($3.27–$13.74 reported for maths-tutor terms in one practitioner analysis); south-east England CPCs run 15–30% above national ([BlogBuster CPC data](https://www.blogbuster.so/cpc-by-industry/Tutoring-Services-seo-blog-tool); [Tutor Scale/Medium](https://medium.com/tutor-scale/are-google-ads-worth-it-c4d869a583c); [Visionary Marketing UK CPC guide](https://visionary-marketing.co.uk/blog/how-much-do-google-ads-cost-uk)). No published CPC specifically for "11 plus tutor" was found, but "11 plus mock exam oxford"-type long-tail terms are exactly where a small budget can work: high intent, low competition (few Oxford-specific mock providers exist). Google Ads suits *capturing existing demand* — good for selling mock day places, weak for creating demand for an unknown tuition brand.
- **Facebook/Meta ads**: multiple first-hand accounts of failure at small budgets. A UK maths tutor documented ads being served overwhelmingly to *other tutors* rather than parents, generating zero leads across repeated £10–£20 campaigns ([Jackpot Maths — "Paying to Reach Peers, Not Parents"](https://www.jackpotmaths.com/2024/01/27/facebook-ads-for-tutors-paying-to-reach-peers-not-parents/)); a MetaFilter thread reports the same "ROI was not there" experience ([Ask MetaFilter](https://ask.metafilter.com/319860/advertising-tutoring-services-through-Facebook-ads)). Meta ads *can* work for a concrete, dated, local event (a mock exam day with a price and a scarcity cap) far better than for "tuition" generally — but treat as an experiment, not a plan.

## 7. Partnerships

- **Schools first, at the right altitude.** Atom's growth flywheel started with one school ([Startups.co.uk](https://startups.co.uk/just-started/atom-learning-jake-okeeffe-and-alex-hatvany/)). For Summit the analogue is Oxfordshire prep/primary schools: offer a free or at-cost "exam experience day" or familiarisation session run on your platform. State primaries around grammar-commuter belts and preps feeding MCS/Oxford High are the targets. One school's endorsement converts the school-gate network wholesale.
- **PTAs and clubs** (see §4): sponsor a fete, donate a mock-day place as a raffle prize — you get newsletter and poster placement inside the exact catchment ([PTA+](https://www.pta.co.uk/running-a-pta/community-engagement/building-business-partnerships/)).
- **Activity clubs / wraparound care**: no strong published evidence found of tutoring firms acquiring via sports/activity clubs; plausible but unproven — deprioritise versus schools/PTAs.
- **Adjacent 11+ businesses**: established mock providers (Chuckra model) license venues and partner with local test centres ([Chuckra venues](https://www.11plus.co.uk/venues/)) — Summit could conversely *be* the Oxford venue/partner for a national brand to borrow credibility, though this risks building their brand locally instead of yours.

## 8. What commonly FAILS at zero customers

- **Broad Facebook/Instagram brand advertising** — documented money-loser at small scale; audience-targeting mis-fires (ads shown to tutors, not parents) ([Jackpot Maths](https://www.jackpotmaths.com/2024/01/27/facebook-ads-for-tutors-paying-to-reach-peers-not-parents/)).
- **Generic Instagram content marketing** — no tutor in any thread reviewed attributed a single client to organic Instagram; the audience (Year 4–5 parents) discovers via search and recommendation, not feeds.
- **Leafleting at school gates** — actively harms reputation with schools and parents ([UK Business Forums](https://www.ukbusinessforums.co.uk/threads/handing-out-leaflets-to-students-outside-of-secondary-schools.366343/)). Letterbox drops in affluent areas *did* work for early Atom, but as a labour-intensive founder grind, not paid distribution ([UKTN](https://www.uktech.news/news/founder-interviews/alex-hatvany-founder-atom-learning-20221209)).
- **Pay-per-lead marketplaces (Bark) as a primary channel** — high reported rates of stale/unresponsive leads ([Sidehustles](https://sidehustles.com/bark-com-review/)); usable only opportunistically.
- **Cold self-promotion inside 11+ parent groups/forums** — against most groups' rules, and the community explicitly distrusts recommendations with "a vested interest" ([elevenplusexams forum norms](https://www.elevenplusexams.co.uk/services/11plus-tutors/finding-a-tutor/)). Getting banned early in the exact communities you need is a real risk.
- **Waiting for word of mouth without seeding it** — the Mumsnet tutor consensus is that organic reputation takes years (one tutor: meaningful recommendations only by year 4) ([Mumsnet](https://www.mumsnet.com/talk/tutoring/5060567-fellow-tutors-where-do-you-advertise)). Premium pricing makes cold trust even harder; proof must be manufactured.

## 9. Zero-customer moves vs scale moves

**Works FIRST (0 → ~30 families):**
1. Paid, realistic GL-style **mock exam day in Oxford** (venue or online-proctored), priced accessibly (£30–£70 is the market band), marked fast, with a premium diagnostic report → tuition upsell. Sell places via Google Ads on Oxford 11+ long-tail terms + local parent groups.
2. **One school/PTA partnership** — free familiarisation session or sponsored prize; newsletter placement (Atom's flywheel, PTA+ mechanics).
3. **Founder-led marketplace seeding** (Superprof/Tutorful/First Tutors + elevenplusexams directory listing) for the first hand-raisers and first public reviews.
4. **Explicit referral engineering** — ask every early family for a review + a named introduction; offer sibling/friend discounts (TTA guidance; Kumon's whole-family retention pattern).
5. **Answering, not advertising, in communities** — genuinely helpful presence on elevenplusexams forum/Mumsnet/local groups under your own name; recommendations then travel by PM, which is how that ecosystem works.

**Only works WITH scale (30+ families):**
- Word of mouth as the dominant channel (needs a critical mass of alumni parents).
- SEO/content moats and brand search.
- Multi-venue mock day calendars with 4 paper sets per season (Chuckra model).
- Meta advertising with enough budget/conversion data for the algorithm to find parents.
- Waiting lists as a marketing asset ("secret tutor" scarcity dynamics).
