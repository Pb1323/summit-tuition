# 06 — Positioning & Next Moves for Summit Tuition

*Synthesis of files 01–05. Written last, deliberately opinionated. Where a claim traces to research, the source file is noted; full citations live in those files.*

## 1. The one-sentence read of the market

There is a proven, sold-out product category (realistic GL-style mock exam days with serious diagnostic reports — file 01, §d) that physically does not exist within 25 miles of Oxford (file 04, §3.3), sold by operators who explicitly refuse to go online (Marie Redmond: in-person only — file 01), in a city with demonstrated premium willingness-to-pay (£70+/hr for remote MCS prep via Owl Tutors — file 02 digest) and no local specialist brand capturing it (file 01, gap 2).

**Summit should launch as "the Oxford 11+ mock exam company", not as a tuition platform that also does mocks.** Tuition is the upsell; the mock is the wedge. Every piece of evidence across all five files points the same way:

- Mocks are a *transactional first purchase* that doesn't require trust in an unknown teacher — parents buy the exam experience, not you (file 05, §3).
- Parents already believe in mocks ("mocks are just as important as the prep") and buy 2–3 per season across providers (file 03, §5; file 05, §3).
- "11 plus mock exam Oxford" is an effectively vacant SERP — the clearest quick win in the entire keyword research (file 04, priority 1).
- The mock report is a personalised sales document for tuition — the conversion mechanism is built into the product (file 05, §3).

## 2. Recommended positioning

> **"Real exam conditions. Honest reports. No hothouse."**
> Summit Tuition is Oxford's 11+ exam specialist: GL-style mock exams under genuine test conditions — in Oxford and online — with fast, statistically honest reports that tell you exactly where your child stands and what to do next, and calm expert tuition to close the gaps.

Three deliberate choices baked into that, each attacking a documented weakness:

1. **Local and specific, not national and generic.** Be the only provider that tells the truth about Oxford: *Oxfordshire has no grammar schools* — the 11+ here means MCS/Oxford High/Headington/Abingdon entry or out-of-county Bucks/Berks grammar applications (file 04, §1). Every ranking competitor page gets this wrong or ignores it. This single fact is your E-E-A-T wedge, your homepage headline territory, and something no London programmatic page can credibly copy. Serve **both tracks under one roof** — no national player is structured to do that (file 01, gap 6).

2. **Honest and calm, not gamified and pressured.** The category leader's documented soft spot is emotional: Atom's volatile scores, ever-present timers and discouraging difficulty stress children out (file 01, §1). Meanwhile the parent community polices over-tutoring hard — "a grammar school isn't worth damaging your relationship with your child" (file 03, §6). Position mocks as *experience + diagnosis + confidence, never prediction* (file 03, §5 insight), and make "statistically stable, transparently explained scores" a named feature. You already enforce no-raw-numbers, review-gated reports in the product — this is that philosophy, marketed.

3. **Premium by report quality, not by price tag.** The market's gold standard is Marie Redmond's 48-hour turnaround with per-question video and cohort ranking, at £80 in-person (file 01, §12). The only online alternative is a £35 unproctored sitting with thin reporting (Chuckra). The whitespace is literally priced: **£50–65 per online invigilated mock with a Redmond-grade report; £70–85 for Oxford in-person days** — between the two anchors, justified by the report (file 02 digest, points 1–2).

## 3. Three messaging directions to test

Test these as landing-page headlines / ad copy against each other. All use verbatim parent language from file 03's language bank.

**A. The exam-experience angle (lead candidate):**
"The 11+ is won on exam technique and timing — not more worksheets. Give your child the real exam experience before the day it counts." Backed by: "the experience of being in a huge hall", "tight time limit", "difference between qualify or not, especially if borderline" (file 03). This sells the mock on the exact grounds parents already use to justify it to each other.

**B. The honest-diagnosis angle:**
"Know exactly where your child stands — before September does it for you. Fast, honest reports: what the score means, what to fix, and what to ignore." Backed by: the "mock disaster — what now?" panic-thread genre and the total absence of commercial content answering "what does the mock score mean" (file 03, §5; file 04, priority 9). This angle owns the post-mock anxiety moment competitors ignore.

**C. The calm-Oxford-insider angle:**
"Oxford has no grammar schools — so what does the 11+ mean here? The local guide, the right preparation, and none of the hothouse." Backed by: the misinformation on ranking pages (file 04, §1), the over-tutoring taboo (file 03, §6), and premium-local vacancy (file 01, gap 2). This is the SEO/content voice even if A or B wins the ad test.

Avoid entirely: score-prediction claims, "guaranteed results", pass-rate boasting, and anything that smells of the arms race — the community's cynicism about "tutor-proof" claims (file 03, §7) extends to provider hype.

## 4. Prioritised next moves (zero customers → first ~30 families)

In order. The first three are the plan; the rest support it.

1. **Put one dated Oxford mock exam day on sale.** One venue, one date (Easter or May half-term, matching the Feb–July mock season — file 03, §5), 30–60 places, £70–80, GL-style with audio instructions, report within 72 hours including percentile vs cohort and a "what this means at this stage" section. This is simultaneously your first revenue, your first 30–60 target families in one room, your first reviews, and your first parent-group mentions (file 05, §3 and §9). Until a venue is booked, sell the **online invigilated version at £50–60** — the product nobody in the UK currently offers (file 01, gap 1).

2. **Ship the five SEO pages that are winnable now** (file 04, priority table): the "11 plus mock exam Oxford" product page; "11 plus tutor Oxford — the honest guide (no grammar schools here)"; MCS + Oxford High + Headington/Abingdon prep pages; "Applying to Bucks & Berkshire grammar schools from Oxfordshire"; "What 11+ tutoring costs in Oxford in 2026". These SERPs are vacant or held by remote generalists and directories. Write them in parent language (file 03 language bank), each with a free GL-style paper as the lead magnet (table-stakes funnel entry — file 02 digest, point 6).

3. **Run a small Google Ads budget on high-intent long-tails only** — "11 plus mock exam oxford", school-specific terms, Bucks-transfer-test-from-Oxfordshire terms — pointed at the mock day page. Google Ads captures existing demand well and this demand has nowhere local to go; skip Meta brand advertising entirely (documented money-loser at small scale, ads served to other tutors — file 05, §6 and §8). A dated, capped, priced local event is the one format where a small Meta test is defensible later.

4. **Land one school or PTA partnership before September.** Offer a free "exam experience" familiarisation session to one prep/primary feeding MCS/Oxford High or the grammar-commuter belt; alternatively sponsor a PTA fete with a mock-day place as raffle prize for newsletter placement (file 05, §7). Atom's founders called their first school the inflection point (file 05, §1). One is enough.

5. **Engineer referrals from family one.** After every early mock: ask for a Google review and a named introduction; offer a sibling discount — almost no competitor advertises one, so it's a cheap visible differentiator (file 02 digest, point 6). The 11+ network is dense and closed ("conspiracy of silence" around good tutors — file 05, §2); 3–5 vouching parents in the right WhatsApp group is compounding growth, and there is no shortcut — cold self-promotion in those groups gets you banned (file 05, §8).

6. **Founder presence, not brand presence, in communities.** Genuinely helpful answers on the Eleven Plus Exams forum and Mumsnet under your own name; recommendations travel by private message in that ecosystem (file 05, §5 and §9). Skip Reddit — no indexed 11+ presence (file 03, §9). Optionally seed 5–10 first tuition families via a founder profile on Superprof/Tutorful, then migrate the goodwill to the Summit brand — but only as a bridge, the take-rates and brand dilution make it a dead end at scale (file 05, §5).

**What to deprioritise, explicitly:** competing on practice-paper head terms (publisher-owned — file 04, §2.1); a cheap subscription to fight BOFA at £9.50/month (race to the bottom against automation); Instagram content; leaflets; and building more platform features before the first mock day sells out. The platform you have — timed GL-style mocks, honest confidence-aware reporting, review-gated releases — is already the product the research says the market lacks. The missing piece is not software; it's the first 30 families, and the mock day is how every successful operator in this niche has manufactured them.

## 5. The pricing architecture this implies

| Product | Price | Anchor logic |
|---|---|---|
| Free GL-style taster paper / diagnostic | £0 | Table-stakes lead magnet (file 02) |
| Online invigilated GL mock + full report | £50–60 | Above Chuckra's £35 unproctored, below in-person; justified by proctoring + report depth |
| Oxford in-person mock day | £70–85 | At/above Redmond's £80 — you're the only local option |
| Mock series (3–4 across the season) | £180–260 | Volume ladder is expected behaviour (£80→£50 pattern — file 02) |
| Small-group tuition programme (weekly + mock series + reports bundled) | £120–180/month | Sits in the empty £25–45/mo-platform ↔ £50+/hr-tutor gap; "£160/month… worth every penny" is a real parent quote (file 03, §4) |
| 1:1 specialist tuition | £55–70/hr | Oxford premium tier, under London-agency £70–120 (file 02) |

Sibling discount on all of it, advertised loudly.

*Bottom line: launch as Oxford's mock exam authority with an honest-report, low-stress premium brand; let the mock day manufacture the first 30 families; let the reports sell the tuition; let the five vacant SEO pages compound. Revisit positioning only after the first two mock cohorts' data tells you whether the tuition attach rate or the mock volume is the business.*
